## Implication

The earlier assumption that Journal of Behavioral Finance could be the lead stable
target is wrong. Its official Taylor & Francis acceptance rate is too low for the
user's 70-80% stability objective.

The corrected interpretation is:

- no verified, reputable SSCI finance/economics journal should be assumed to have
  a 70-80% single-journal acceptance rate;
- the only defensible 70-80% number is project-level eventual acceptance after a
  carefully sequenced submission ladder;
- Applied Economics Letters is currently the best verified first target because it
  is SSCI Q3, scope-compatible, short-format friendly, and has a published 35%
  acceptance rate.

## Corrected Submission Strategy

Primary target:

1. Applied Economics Letters
   - Paper form: concise empirical letter.
   - Core claim: analyst forecast dispersion/revisions provide incremental warning
     for future financial stress/distress in Asia-Pacific listed firms.
   - Maximum paper complexity: logistic regression, XGBoost, SHAP, robustness.

Stretch target only:

2. Journal of Behavioral Finance
   - Use only if the final manuscript has a strong behavioral-finance mechanism
     and the user accepts low single-journal odds.
   - Do not use it as the lead route for a stability-first plan.

Further verified backups:

3. Select after pilot data results are known, using JCR filters:
   - Edition must be SSCI.
   - JIF quartile should be Q3, or Q2 only if the target accepts higher-tier
     journals as acceptable.
   - Scope must accept empirical applied economics, financial economics, or risk
     prediction papers.
   - Official acceptance rate should be recorded if the publisher discloses it.

## Hard Rule

Do not submit to an ESCI journal if the user's requirement remains strictly SSCI.
Do not describe a journal as "stable 70-80%" unless the publisher itself publishes
that acceptance rate.
